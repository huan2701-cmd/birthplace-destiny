Part I

The American Story

The Opportunity Atlas tracked 20 million Americans born 1978–1992 to reveal how geography shapes economic destiny.

Data: Raj Chetty et al., Harvard Opportunity Insights

Finding 1

The Geography of Opportunity

Expected income rank for children from the poorest families, by county (1978 cohort)

Hover over any county. Scroll to zoom. Drag to pan.

For children born into the poorest families, geography is destiny. In parts of the Upper Midwest, a child from the bottom reaches the 55th percentile by their mid-30s. In the Deep South, they barely reach the 35th. Same country, same era, vastly different outcomes.

Finding 2

Rich Kids vs Poor Kids

Outcome percentile for children from the poorest (x) vs richest (y) families, by county

Hover over any county to see the gap between rich and poor children.

Every dot above the diagonal represents a county where children from wealthy families outperform those from poor families. Nearly every county in America is above that line. But the distance — the gap between rich and poor kids — varies enormously.

Finding 3

Which States Are Getting Better?

Change in upward mobility between the 1978 and 1992 birth cohorts, by state

Hover over any state for details.

The geography of opportunity is not static. Some states saw meaningful improvements in mobility between the 1978 and 1992 birth cohorts. Others saw the ladder get harder to climb.

Within the United States, geography is destiny for poor families.

But zoom out — and the same pattern plays out between countries.

Part II

The Global Story

Countries with nearly identical market inequality end up with vastly different outcomes — depending on whether they choose to redistribute.

Data: SWIID (Solt 2020), World Inequality Database, Our World in Data

Finding 4

The Redistribution Gap

Market Gini (○ before tax) vs Disposable Gini (● after tax). Source: SWIID v9.0

Hover over any country. Click a region in the legend to filter.

Before taxes, most developed countries look remarkably similar (Gini 43–50). After redistribution, the picture diverges. Nordic countries cut inequality nearly in half; the US barely budges.

Finding 5

Where Does the Money Go?

If all national income were $100, how would it be divided? Source: World Inequality Database, ~2022

Hover on any segment to compare that income group across all countries.

In South Africa, the bottom half gets $8 out of every $100. In Norway, they get $26. The United States — the world's largest economy — gives its poorest half only $13.

Finding 6

What Inequality Costs

Top 10% income share vs life expectancy. Sources: WID, Our World in Data, World Bank

Hover to explore. Click legend to filter by region.

As the top 10% takes more, life expectancy drops. The US is the starkest outlier — the richest large economy, but with life expectancy below Chile.

The bottom line

Inequality isn't destiny.
It's a policy choice.

Within the U.S., a child's county shapes their future more than almost any other factor. Across countries, similar levels of market inequality produce vastly different outcomes depending on whether governments choose to redistribute. The data shows that where you are born matters enormously — but it doesn't have to.

Data: Opportunity Atlas (Chetty et al.), SWIID (Solt 2020), World Inequality Database, Our World in Data
